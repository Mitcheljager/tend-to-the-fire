## General notes

- Dying isn't fun. Once you die you will be forced to replay bits that were scary the first time, but now that you've experienced them they are just a means to an end, that end being getting back to the bit you died on. If that happens over and over again, that's just frustrating. That's not to say the player shouldn't be able to die. Having been alive for a long while builds it's own tension. Knowing you will have to replay the game if you fail is a good motivator to stay alive. That want to stay alive should be encouraged. Allow the player to die, but only in a fair way. Create high stakes with many ways out.
- No gore. Gore is not scary, it can come across as more of a comedic relief unless it's actually scary looking. With the PSX style graphic we're going for that just isn't happening. Gore will always look cheesy.
- Horror often follows a predictable pattern. Build up, scary, followed by relief. It's a constant flow, but is that actually scary? The moment of relief will start to feel expected and that takes away from the scares themselves. The goal should not be to get to that point of relief. In the early game we might want to give players moments like this, but as the game goes on the player should start to feel constantly on edge. Always fleeing, always on the look out without having a safe heaven to retreat to. There should be moments where a player thinks they might feel safe, but we should break that at some points. Sometimes breaking those moments might be enough to keep the player on edge at all points. If they weren't safe that one time, they might also not be this time.
- No UI, let the player immerse themselves in the world. Health and stamina (if we end up using those) can be conveyed through screen effects. Exceptions are subtitles, objectives, hints, etc.; anything that can't be conveyed without text. Ideally these exceptions only occur on player interaction. Text shouldn't "just" appear and risk breaking tensions.
- The player's face is not a camera. No lens flares, chromatic abberation, or others effects that don't make sense to your eyes.

## Styles notes

- Graphics are a combination of PSX and modern styles. PSX styles are used for models and textures, but lighting might use more modern effects. We also shouldn't feel limited by limits of the PSX era. If dense level geometry makes sense than let it be so. The style should be unified and consistent, but not necessarily accurate to a specific period.
- Sounds plays from realistic directions, playing from corners rather than from their "real" position.
- Film grain might show shapes?
- Creature has no visible shape at all. Fear of the unknown and lack of animations are alluring, but how do we make it scary still?
- Perhaps the creature can be identified by a blur? Camera noise of some kind?
- Story is told from the characters recollection, like he's telling a story. "I used to browse this website", "Finally I found the cave", "X and x happened".

## Gameplay moments

- The being attempts to lead the player towards a goal. If the player follows their direction all is well. If the player attempts to explore or backtrack the being will start to fight back, frightening the player towards their set goal.
- Footsteps that follow the player, almost mimicking their own footsteps. As the player stops so do the footsteps.
- Something appears when you close your eyes.
- When the player "wins" they enter into a mirrored world. This should be subtle.
- Infinitely looping environments. Sometimes scary things might appear in one or all of the loops.
- Use instructions as scares? "F for flashlight" is simple enough. "Shift to run" gives an uneasy feeling, but what if we go further. "G to scare the beast", "Z to give up all hope", "P to succumb to fear". These controls wouldn't do anything but mess with the player. This is only scary once, of course. We could go all out with it. Have prompts appear as the player is running away from some being. Start innocent but build it up until the screen is full of prompt. Only for them to vanish, for the whole world to turn black as your flashlight turns off. Followed by a "F for flashlight" prompt that brings the player back into the world.
- Endless human shapes stand in an endless hall, forever out of reach.
- Crawling through a tight space as something follows you?
- The creature moves very fast but can be heard from quite far away. This means there's little time to spot the create and if you do spot it you will need to hide quickly, as it could move to much faster than you can get away.
- A hallway with flickering lights. Might give a similar effect to a train car. Each time the light flickers something might happen or show for a split second. Might only be effective if the player has no flashlight. Maybe it's the shadows that flicker, rather than the lights, overpowering the flashlight. Or maybe the flashlight flickers along with the lights in the hallway.
- An infinitely looping walkway in which you can see yourself walk across. As you watch a creature appears on the copied loops, but not on your own. The scary could come from realizing the creature is right behind you, except it isn't really. Might need the walkway to be lit by natural light, but the player's flashlight might enough.
- A mysterious floating planet-y orb that changes every time you look at it. Why? I don't know, it sounds cool.
- Some infinite hallway or shaft where each iterating some monster makes itself more and more apparent.
- A long hallway that at the end has something pop up that requires you to run back all the way to avoid it.
- A scene of endless water. As the player wades in far enough someone starts to chase them with a torment of splashes. More and more splashes appear from all directions until they reach the player and pull them under with no escape.
- As the player wals along a hallway they hear something behind them. First just a scattering of rock, then footsteps. Footsteps only play as the player walks forward. At some point the slow footsteps turn into a full on sprint, always reaching the player no matter what. Nothing happens.
- A doorway in the middle of an empty, perhaps infinite, room. In the middle of this infinity stands a doorway with stairs leading up to it. The doorway shows an endless vista of stars. There's nothing to do here except enbrace the moment of tranquility and temporary respite.
